This hub introduces the primary languages of the Bible—Hebrew, Greek, and Aramaic—and gathers links to resources that help you explore the text in its original forms. -AI

Why biblical languages matter

The Old and New Testaments were written in ancient languages whose words, grammar, and idioms sometimes carry nuance that can be flattened or obscured in translation; studying these languages can clarify meanings, patterns, and connections across Scripture. -AI

On this site, biblical language resources are designed to be approachable starting points: you can follow Strong’s numbers, compare translations, or simply read brief notes and examples without needing formal language training. -AI

Hebrew

Biblical Hebrew is the primary language of the Old Testament, a Semitic language characterized by its consonantal roots and rich poetic and narrative styles. -AI

Here you will find Hebrew-oriented resources such as Strong’s Hebrew entries, Hebrew-based translations, and future tools for exploring key words and phrases across the Old Testament. -AI

Greek

The New Testament was written in Koine Greek, the common dialect of the eastern Mediterranean in the first century, combining precise grammatical structure with a flexible vocabulary. -AI

Greek resources on this site include Strong’s Greek entries, Greek-based translations, and future study tools aimed at tracing words, themes, and grammatical patterns in the New Testament. -AI

Aramaic

Aramaic, closely related to Hebrew, appears in a few Old Testament sections and in certain sayings preserved in the New Testament, reflecting the everyday spoken language of many Jews in the Second Temple period. -AI

Aramaic resources here will highlight key Aramaic passages, terms, and connections to the surrounding Hebrew and Greek texts, with room to grow into more detailed lexical and grammatical tools over time. -AI
